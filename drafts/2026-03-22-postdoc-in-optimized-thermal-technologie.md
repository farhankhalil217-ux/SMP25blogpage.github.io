# Postdoc in Optimized thermal technologies for industrial decarbonization at KTH

The global imperative to decarbonize industrial processes represents one of the most significant challenges and opportunities of our era. As industries from manufacturing to chemicals seek viable pathways to sustainable operations, solar energy, particularly through advanced thermal technologies, is emerging as a critical enabler. While much of the recent discourse in the solar investment community centers on photovoltaic (PV) deployment, the sophisticated engineering behind high-temperature solar thermal applications for process heat demands an equally rigorous and data-driven approach to performance modeling and risk assessment.

## Beyond the Hardware: Optimizing Performance Prediction

The ongoing advancements in "optimized thermal technologies for industrial decarbonization" — as highlighted by cutting-edge research initiatives — are a testament to the industry's commitment to innovation. Yet, true optimization extends far beyond the physical components of a solar thermal plant. It necessitates an equally advanced and dynamic understanding of how these systems will perform under real-world, variable conditions, and critically, how that performance translates into predictable financial outcomes for asset managers and investors.

Traditional performance modeling, often relying on static assumptions and historical averages, falls demonstrably short in capturing the nuances of complex solar thermal systems. Just as with PV, the bankability and long-term viability of solar thermal projects hinge on robust P50 and P90 probability analyses. This isn't merely about forecasting energy output; it's about quantifying the uncertainty inherent in a system whose performance is intrinsically linked to dynamic solar resource availability and intricate thermodynamic processes.

## The Common Denominator: Precision Resource Assessment

Whether designing a utility-scale PV farm or an industrial solar thermal plant, the fundamental building block of any accurate performance model is the solar resource itself. Generic satellite data, while a good starting point, is insufficient for the granular analysis required for optimizing advanced thermal systems. These applications often have specific geographic and atmospheric sensitivities. High-resolution, physics-calibrated satellite data, rigorously validated against ground measurements, becomes non-negotiable.

Consider the impact of localized atmospheric conditions, aerosols, or even seasonal variations in direct normal irradiance (DNI) – a key metric for many thermal systems. Without precise, dynamic resource inputs, any subsequent performance simulation is built on a shaky foundation, directly impacting projected energy yields, operational expenditure (OpEx), and ultimately, the Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) or LCOE. This imprecision introduces unnecessary risk for investors and makes robust project financing challenging.

## Challenging the Status Quo in Performance Engineering

The push for "optimized thermal technologies" offers a crucial opportunity for the solar industry to collectively re-evaluate its modeling paradigms across the board. The same principles that Solar Metrix champions for PV yield simulation — physics-driven modeling, dynamic performance coefficients, and a holistic view of system interactions — are equally vital for thermal applications.

For instance, the sophisticated control strategies and thermal energy storage mechanisms characteristic of modern solar thermal plants introduce complexities that static models simply cannot handle. Predicting how a system will respond to transient cloud cover, manage charge/discharge cycles, or integrate seamlessly with existing industrial processes requires a dynamic, time-series simulation approach. Simplistic "derate factor" methodologies risk underestimating energy capture, miscalculating parasitic losses, and inaccurately projecting O&M costs over the project lifecycle.

Asset managers need confidence not just in the hardware's theoretical capability, but in its ability to consistently deliver optimized output. This confidence can only come from models that accurately account for:

*   **Dynamic Resource Variability:** Leveraging advanced solar resource data, far beyond basic averages.
*   **System-Specific Efficiencies:** Modeling the complex optical, thermal, and thermodynamic conversions with high fidelity.
*   **Operational Constraints:** Accounting for startup/shutdown cycles, part-load performance, and integration limits.
*   **Environmental Factors:** While concepts like PV albedo might seem distinct, the principle of environmental interaction (e.g., dust deposition on heliostats, impact of wind on convection losses) demands similar rigorous modeling attention for thermal systems.

As we move towards a future where industrial decarbonization is not just an aspiration but an economic necessity, the role of advanced solar technologies will only grow. To truly capitalize on this potential, our industry must move beyond outdated, simplified modeling approaches. Whether it's predicting the energy capture of a multi-gigawatt PV plant or ensuring the optimal performance of a high-temperature solar thermal facility, the investment community demands, and deserves, the most accurate, physics-driven performance intelligence available. The era of static spreadsheets must yield to dynamic, data-rich simulations that unlock the full economic and environmental potential of solar innovation.
